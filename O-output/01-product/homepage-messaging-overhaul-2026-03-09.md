## Dark Section (The Acquisition Trap)

### Before
> **Eyebrow:** The question nobody wants to answer
> **Headline:** When did you last experience your product *the way your customers do?*
> **Body:** Not during a demo. Not during onboarding with you on the call. The real experience — when they're stuck, when support is slow, when the renewal conversation catches them off guard. CX Mate maps what actually happens.
> **CTA:** See My Customer's Journey →

### After
> **Eyebrow:** The Acquisition Trap
> **Headline:** You spent months closing the deal. *Then what?*
> **Body:** Most B2B startups pour everything into acquisition — and hope the post-sale experience holds together. It doesn't. The handoff is rough. Onboarding drifts. Support is reactive. The customer starts deciding whether to stay long before renewal.
> **Secondary:** CX Mate maps the experience your customers actually have — the moments you designed, the ones you didn't, and the ones that are quietly costing you revenue.
> **CTA:** See What My Customers Actually Experience →

**Why:** "The Acquisition Trap" is a named concept from our intel research — it gives the problem an identity. The old version asked a question; the new one tells a story that every founder recognizes. The post-sale failure sequence (handoff → onboarding → support → renewal) mirrors exactly what CX Mate diagnoses.

## Comparison Section

### Before
> **Eyebrow:** Why now, not later
> **Headline:** You don't need to wait until Series B to get your CX right
> **Framing:** CX Consultant (Series B+) vs CX Mate (Day One →)
> **Included:** Stage timeline bar showing when companies start thinking about CX
> **Closing:** "CX Mate turns those instincts into a structured plan"

### After
> **Eyebrow:** The intelligence gap
> **Headline:** Your CX stack collects data. CX Mate tells you what to do with it.
> **Framing:** Traditional CX Tools vs CX Mate
> **Comparison rows:** What you get / Time to value / Cost / The gap / Who it's for
> **Quote:** "Overwhelmed by data but underwhelmed by guidance on what to do next." — How users describe their current CX tools
> **Closing:** CX Mate isn't another dashboard to stare at. **It's the diagnosis your team actually acts on.**

**Why:** The consultant comparison was respectful but off-target — our ICP isn't choosing between us and a consultant. They're choosing between us and the CX tools they already have (or don't have). The new framing directly addresses the intelligence gap that Perplexity research surfaced. The quote is from real Qualtrics user reviews — it's the pain we solve.

**Removed:** The stage timeline bar. It was conceptually nice but added visual complexity without driving conversion. The new section is tighter and more confrontational.

## Features Section

### Before
> **Eyebrow:** What you walk away with
> **Cards:** Feature titles + descriptions
> - Journey Map: "Your customer lifecycle, end to end"
> - CX Playbook: "A plan you can actually execute"
> - CX Review: "Ready for your next management meeting"
> - Live Intelligence: "Specific to you. Never generic."

### After
> **Eyebrow:** What founders actually ask
> **Cards:** Questions + answers
> - Journey Map: "Where are customers dropping off?"
> - CX Playbook: "What should my team do first?"
> - CX Review: "How do I present this to leadership?"
> - Live Intelligence: "How is this different from a template?"

**Why:** Reframing features as questions does two things: (1) it mirrors how founders actually think about their problems, and (2) it makes each card feel like a direct answer rather than a product spec. The reader sees their own question, then gets the answer. Much more engaging than feature-title → description.
