## Part 2: Copy Principles for the AI Prompt

These rules govern every piece of generated copy in the analysis output. Embed them in the generation prompt.

---

### Rule 1: Sentence Length Cap
- Max 20 words per sentence.
- If a sentence needs a comma, it probably needs a period instead.
- Exception: data-rich sentences that list specifics ("transaction growth, uptime, cost savings") can run to 25.

### Rule 2: Active Voice, No Exceptions
- Every sentence has a subject that does something.
- "Revenue is being lost" becomes "You're losing revenue."
- "Expansion happens only when customers request" becomes "Customers drive expansion — you don't."
- Test: if you can add "by zombies" after the verb and it makes grammatical sense, it's passive. Rewrite it.

### Rule 3: Word Economy
Cut these on sight:

| Kill | Replace with |
|------|-------------|
| in order to | to |
| is able to | can |
| due to the fact that | because |
| at the present time | now |
| a large number of | many |
| the vast majority of | most |
| on a daily basis | daily |
| in the event that | if |
| has the ability to | can |
| it is important to note that | (delete entirely) |
| as a result of | from / because |
| in terms of | (delete or restructure) |
| with regard to | about / for |
| it should be noted that | (delete entirely) |
| the fact that | that |
| currently | (delete — present tense implies it) |
| basically / essentially | (delete — if it's basic, show it) |

### Rule 4: No Hedging in Findings
- Never: "This may indicate" / "There could potentially be" / "It appears that"
- Always: "This shows" / "Your data reveals" / "The pattern is clear"
- Confidence is the product. Caveats belong in the methodology footnote, not the finding.

### Rule 5: Specificity Over Adjectives
- "Significant revenue impact" tells nothing. "$120K at risk" tells everything.
- "Several key issues" is vague. "4 blind spots" is concrete.
- If you wrote an adjective, ask: can I replace this with a number, a name, or a tool? If yes, do it.

### Rule 6: Front-Load the Impact
- Lead with what changed, what's broken, or what it costs. Context comes second.
- Bad: "Due to the lack of a proactive renewal process, Vendora is at risk of losing $75K annually."
- Good: "$75K at risk because renewals start with an email 30 days out — nothing before that."

### Rule 7: One Idea Per Paragraph
- If a paragraph makes two points, split it.
- The reader is scanning. Give them one thing to absorb per block.

### Rule 8: Mirror the User's Language
- Use the exact pain point phrases from onboarding input.
- If they said "onboarding drop-off," write "onboarding drop-off" — not "user activation challenges."
- This is how you prove the output is theirs, not a template.

## Part 3: Headline Formulas

---

### Blind Spot Headlines

**Pattern:** [Concrete Problem] + [Quantified Impact or Specific Detail]

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| Silent Adoption Failure | Silent Adoption Failure — 40% Never Activate |
| Expansion Is Entirely Reactive | Zero Proactive Expansion — $120K Left on the Table |
| Renewal Issues Detected | Renewals Run on Calendar Reminders, Not Value Proof |
| Onboarding Needs Improvement | Onboarding Ends at Day 3 — Then Nothing |
| Support Is Reactive | Support Waits for Tickets Instead of Preventing Them |

**Rules:**
1. The headline alone should make someone pause. No body text needed.
2. Include either a number, a dollar amount, or a time reference.
3. Use a dash to attach the detail to the problem.
4. Never use "issues" or "challenges" — name the actual problem.

---

### Action Item Headlines

**Pattern:** [Verb] + [Specific Tool or Asset] + [in/for Context]

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| Improve Onboarding | Build an Activation Sequence in Intercom |
| Address Expansion | Wire Expansion Triggers in HubSpot |
| Fix Renewal Process | Launch a Quarterly Merchant Impact Report |

**Rules:**
1. Start with a strong verb: Build, Wire, Launch, Set up, Create, Configure.
2. Name the tool. Always. "Build a workflow" is generic. "Build a HubSpot workflow" is actionable.
3. The reader should know what to build, where, without reading the details.

---

### The Confrontation Headline — The Most Important Line on the Page

This is the single line that makes a founder screenshot the analysis and send it to their co-founder. It must hit three notes: scope (how many issues), cost (dollar impact), and personal proof (connection to what they told us).

**Current version:**
> Your customer journey has 4 critical blind spots costing Vendora an estimated $340K annually. Three of these trace directly back to the pain points you told us about: onboarding drop-off, silent churn, and expansion friction.

---

**Variation 1: Lead with the number, punch with the proof**
> 4 blind spots. $340K at risk. Three match the exact pain points you flagged.

Why it works: Staccato rhythm. Each fragment is a punch. "Exact pain points you flagged" mirrors the user's input back — proof of personalization in seven words.

---

**Variation 2: Lead with the money, close with the mirror**
> Vendora is leaving $340K on the table across 4 blind spots — and three of them are the problems you already suspected.

Why it works: "Already suspected" validates the founder's instinct. It says: you were right, and now you have proof. "Leaving on the table" implies agency — this is fixable.

---

**Variation 3: Lead with the confrontation, back it with data**
> You told us about onboarding drop-off, silent churn, and expansion friction. We found all three — plus a fourth you haven't spotted yet. Total cost: $340K/year.

Why it works: Opens by quoting the user's own words back. "Plus a fourth you haven't spotted yet" adds discovery value — CX Mate found something the founder didn't know. Ends with the dollar amount as the period on the sentence.

---

**Recommendation:** Use Variation 3 as the default. It has the strongest personalization signal (opens with their words), adds surprise value (the fourth blind spot), and anchors with cost. It makes the founder feel both validated and challenged — which is the exact emotional state that drives action.
