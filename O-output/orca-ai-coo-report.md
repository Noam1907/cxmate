## The Core Challenge

**You are not selling AI. You are asking experienced maritime professionals to trust a machine with their safety decisions.**

Every CX failure at Orca AI ultimately traces back to this: the product challenges the professional identity of the people who have to use it. Bridge officers with 20 years of sea experience don't naturally want an AI watching them. Fleet safety directors don't know how to quantify the absence of an accident. CFOs don't have a line item for "prevented collisions."

The CX challenge is not technical. It is cultural, psychological, and commercial — all at once.

The companies that solve this don't do it by improving the product. They do it by engineering the experience around how their customers actually think about their own professional identity and risk.

## CX Assessment: Six Critical Patterns

*Ranked by likelihood and revenue impact. Each pattern includes: what's happening, how to detect it, and what to do this week.*

### 1. The Safety Culture Wall
**Severity: Critical · Likelihood: 95%**

**What's happening:**

Your bridge officers have 15–25 years of professional experience reading the ocean. They didn't reach Officer of the Watch by needing software to monitor their attention. When Orca AI is introduced using language like "monitoring system" or framed around gaps in human watchkeeping, it triggers a defensive response that is entirely rational from their perspective.

The system gets installed. It doesn't get used.

This isn't user adoption friction — it's professional identity protection. And it's happening silently. Installation metrics look fine. Adoption data tells a different story.

**How to detect it:**
- Alert acknowledgment rate below 70% on installed vessels after 30 days
- Bridge officers describing the system as "optional" to new crew members
- Shore-side reports of system being set to silent mode or minimum sensitivity
- Bridge team feedback: "We know our routes. This is for newer officers."

*Keren Shaked's approach to startup CX applies directly here: before building any customer engagement process, ask what the customer actually needs to succeed — not what you think looks like good service. What bridge officers need is for their professional judgment to be visibly respected. The product framing has to change before the product training begins.*

**Revenue at risk:**

A 20-vessel fleet at $8,000/vessel/year = $160K ARR. When adoption fails on a first fleet, the reference story fails. No referrals. No expansion.

Estimated ARR at risk from first-fleet adoption failure: **$160K direct + $400K expansion opportunity = $560K.**

**Immediate action this week:**

Audit every deployment document and customer-facing communication that contains the word "monitoring" when addressed to bridge officers. Rewrite it. Replace with: *"mission augmentation," "an extra set of eyes on sectors outside your primary attention window," "your watch, with better peripheral awareness."*

This one language change has more impact on adoption than any feature improvement.

### 2. The Phantom Metric Problem
**Severity: Critical · Likelihood: 90%**

**What's happening:**

You are selling the absence of accidents. The renewal conversation arrives at Month 11 with: *"It's been running smoothly. We think it's been useful."* That sentence does not survive a CFO budget review. In maritime enterprise, every line item above $50K is reviewed.

Without instrumenting near-miss event frequency, alert response rates, and incident severity data from Day 1 on every vessel, there is no data to defend the renewal — because no one thought to capture the baseline.

*Ian Golding (CCXP) identifies this as a universal failure mode: "CX strategy without measurement is just good intentions. Every initiative needs a metric, a baseline, and a target." Right now, Orca AI has the initiative. Most customers don't yet have the baseline or the target.*

**The four metrics to capture from Day 1:**
1. Near-miss events detected (system-logged, categorised by risk level)
2. Alert acknowledgment rate per vessel per week
3. System uptime % per vessel
4. Pre-deployment baseline: near-miss reports filed in the 12 months before installation (ask the safety department on Day 1 — this data is inaccessible at Day 300)

*Bain & Company research: customers who can articulate ROI renew at 6× the rate of those who cannot. At 10 fleet customers renewing at $160K average, the difference between "can articulate ROI" and "can't" represents potentially $800K+ in preventable churn annually as the company scales.*

**Immediate action this week:**

For every active fleet customer, schedule a 30-minute baseline capture call. Ask: "Before we installed the system, what did your near-miss reporting look like? What incident costs did you carry last year?" This data, recorded in your CRM today, is your renewal business case in 12 months.

### 3. The MEDDPICC Black Hole
**Severity: Critical · Likelihood: 80%**

**What's happening:**

Your sales cycles run 6–18 months across 4–6 stakeholders. Somewhere in that process, critical context is lost — specifically: *what metric did you commit to delivering, to whom, and by when?*

The Fleet Safety Director who approved the purchase cares about regulatory compliance and insurance cost. The COO cares about total cost of ownership. The bridge officers care about operational reliability. None of these three people told the CS team what they actually care about — because the handoff document doesn't exist or doesn't capture it.

When CS inherits an account without structured qualification notes, they are managing a relationship they don't fully understand. The renewal conversation is guesswork. The champion departure is a surprise. The expansion motion has no business case to build from.

**The minimum handoff required at every contract close:**

| Field | What to capture for Orca AI |
|-------|----------------------------|
| **Metrics** | What safety KPI is the fleet director trying to move? (Near-miss rate? Insurance cost? TMSA score?) |
| **Economic Buyer** | Who signed? Who will sign the renewal? |
| **Decision Criteria** | What was evaluated? What almost made them choose another vendor? |
| **Decision Process** | How was the decision made? Same process will govern the renewal. |
| **Identified Pain** | Was it regulatory pressure? An incident? A board mandate? This is the renewal anchor. |
| **Champion** | Name, title, tenure, LinkedIn URL. This person leaving = churn risk. |
| **Critical Event** | Is there a regulatory audit, insurance renewal, or board review that drove the timeline? |

*If sales closes a deal without structured notes, CS inherits a ghost account.*

**Immediate action this week:**

For every current customer, open the CRM record and answer: "What metric did we commit to improving?" If you cannot answer in 30 seconds, the renewal is at risk. Schedule a call framed as: "We want to make sure we're tracking the right things for your fleet" — use it to capture the baseline and context that should have been captured at contract signing.

### 4. The Post-Installation Desert
**Severity: High · Likelihood: 85%**

**What's happening:**

Hardware is installed. Training is delivered. The system goes live. Then: silence.

No structured 30-day vessel check-in. No adoption monitoring per vessel. No quarterly safety report. No proactive outreach until the renewal conversation 11 months later. The system runs quietly in the background, and when renewal arrives, no one can tell the story of what it delivered.

*Shep Hyken's research identifies this directly: the period between customer touchpoints — when the absence of contact is itself a Moment of Misery. The customer interprets silence as indifference.* In maritime, where fleet directors are busy, hierarchical, and unlikely to chase a vendor, this interpretation compounds into a political decision to cancel that forms 6 months before the renewal conversation.

*Industry research: 80% of churn at renewal was predictable 90+ days in advance. For every customer who complains, 26 stay silent and churn. In maritime enterprise, that ratio is likely worse.*

**The 90-day vessel health protocol:**

- **Day 7 post-installation:** System uptime confirmed. Bridge officer questions addressed. Any bypass behaviour flagged.
- **Day 30:** Vessel adoption scorecard — alert acknowledgment rate, uptime %, officer feedback. Shared proactively with fleet director.
- **Day 60:** First near-miss events report. "Here's what we've seen on your vessels so far."
- **Day 90:** First Quarterly Safety Intelligence Report — the document that goes to the C-suite and establishes the data story for renewal.

**Immediate action this week:**

Pull a list of every active fleet customer. For any customer where there has been no structured outreach in the last 45 days — schedule one this week. Frame it as: "We're building a quarterly safety intelligence process for all fleets and want to make sure you're included from the start."

### 5. The Champion Departure Crisis
**Severity: High · Likelihood: 80%**

**What's happening:**

Maritime operations leadership turns over. Fleet Safety Directors average 2–3 year tenures. Maritime Superintendents rotate between roles and companies. When the person who championed the Orca AI purchase leaves — for retirement, promotion, or a role at another shipping group — the new person inherits a line item they don't understand.

They've seen the invoices. They haven't seen the near-miss reports. They don't know why this was purchased. And no one from Orca AI proactively onboards them.

*Research confirms: when the sole champion departs, churn probability triples within 6 months.*

*Jeanne Bliss's work on customer-centric leadership identifies the CEO review of customer loss patterns as a critical retention lever. At Orca AI's stage, the CEO needs to know when a champion is at risk — before they leave, not after.*

**The 14-Day Rescue Protocol:**

| Timing | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Day 1–3 | CS Manager sends warm, personal message to departing Champion requesting 20-minute handoff call |
| Day 3–7 | Identify successor. Schedule "New Fleet Leadership Briefing" — 45 minutes: why the purchase was made, what it's delivered, what comes next |
| Day 7–14 | Prepare "Why We Chose Orca AI" one-pager, co-authored with departing Champion where possible. Leave in fleet's shared documentation. |
| Day 30 | Follow-up with new leader: "What do you need from us to feel confident in this program?" |

**Immediate action this week:**

For every current fleet customer, name the Champion and find them on LinkedIn. Enable job change notifications on their profile. This takes 15 minutes per account. Do it today.

### 6. The Fleet Bottleneck
**Severity: Medium · Likelihood: 60%**

**What's happening:**

Hardware deployment is operationally constrained in ways software deployments aren't. Vessels are at sea for 6–8 weeks at a time. Dry dock schedules are booked 6–12 months in advance. Maritime electricians are scarce in certain ports. A deployment plan that ignores the operational calendar creates delays that defer revenue and erode champion goodwill.

The bottleneck is rarely technical — it is logistical. And when deployment takes 18 months instead of 6, the business case built during the sales process expires before the fleet is fully live.

**Revenue impact of deployment delay:**

Each month of delay = 1/12 of vessel-year revenue deferred + compounding champion fatigue. For a 20-vessel fleet at $160K/year: a 6-month delay = $80K deferred, plus material risk of champion disengagement.

**Immediate action this week:**

Create a Fleet Deployment Operations Checklist that captures, for each vessel: next port date, next dry dock date, current route, crew rotation schedule. Build this into the sales contract as a joint deployment plan. The first evidence a fleet director sees of Orca AI's operational competence should be this document.

## Customer Journey Map
*Full lifecycle — 7 stages, 35 meaningful moments*

---

### Stage 1: Strategic Awareness
**Goal:** Be present when a shipping company decides to act on navigational AI.
**Customer feeling:** Cautiously curious. Professionally sceptical.
**Critical question:** "Is this proven enough to trust on a 100,000-ton vessel with 20 crew?"

Key moments: Maritime safety conference presence · Industry publication coverage · Peer referral from fleet director · IMO/EMSA regulatory signal triggering internal review · Insurance broker raising incident cost exposure.

**What needs to be true:** Orca AI's credibility story must be built around safety outcomes and Lloyd's-grade data — not AI features. Early adopter fleet results, with specific near-miss data cited (with permission), are the strongest credibility signal available.

---

### Stage 2: Enterprise Evaluation
**Goal:** Become the preferred option against legacy radar and integrated maritime AI competitors.
**Customer feeling:** Analytically cautious. Seeking proof, not promises.
**Critical question:** "What happens if it's wrong? Who is liable?"

Key moments: Technical review with maritime superintendent · Competitive evaluation against Wärtsilä/ABB Marine · Safety and compliance review · Insurance broker assessment · Reference call with existing fleet customer · Pilot scope negotiation.

**What needs to be true:** Every reference customer should be equipped with a data-ready story they can share. The "What happens if it's wrong?" question must be answered proactively — not left for procurement to raise.

---

### Stage 3: Procurement & Contracting
**Goal:** Close the deal with full stakeholder alignment — including the people who will use it.
**Customer feeling:** Committed in principle. Navigating internal process.
**Critical question:** "Can we make this work with our fleet calendar?"

Key moments: Legal and compliance review · Insurance implications assessed · Budget allocation agreed · Vessel deployment schedule drafted · **MEDDPICC handoff document completed** (mandatory before CS kickoff is scheduled) · Contract signing.

**What needs to be true:** The MEDDPICC Handoff Document (see Action 6 in playbook) is complete before CS is introduced. The deployment schedule is built around the fleet's operational calendar, not Orca AI's install team availability.

---

### Stage 4: Vessel Installation ⚠️ Highest-Risk Stage
**Goal:** First vessel goes live with bridge officer buy-in — not just hardware installed.
**Customer feeling:** Bridge officers: sceptical, watching. Fleet director: invested, anxious.
**Critical question (bridge officer):** "Is this going to get in the way of how I run my watch?"

Key moments: Pre-installation fleet director briefing · Fleet director sends bridge officer introduction memo (prepared by Orca AI, sent by director) · Installation day — first officer impression · Initial alert calibration based on officer feedback · Day 7 system uptime confirmation · Day 30 adoption scorecard.

**What needs to be true:** The bridge officer's first impression of this system is shaped by their own leadership, not by a vendor. The introduction must come from the fleet director. The language must respect professional authority completely. (See Enablement Pack — Stage 4 for prepared templates.)

---

### Stage 5: Operational Adoption
**Goal:** The system becomes part of the bridge watch routine — not an optional add-on.
**Customer feeling:** Fleet director: monitoring. Bridge officers: adjusting. CS team: proactively engaged.
**Critical question:** "Is this actually changing how we operate?"

Key moments: Weekly alert acknowledgment review by CS · Configuration adjustments based on officer feedback · Day 60 near-miss event summary · Day 90 Quarterly Safety Intelligence Report to C-suite · "Maritime Safety Champion" officer recognition (named in report) · Customer health score reviewed internally.

**What needs to be true:** By Day 90, the fleet director has received a Quarterly Safety Intelligence Report that makes the renewal obvious. By Day 90, there is at least one bridge officer on each vessel who considers themselves an advocate — named, recognised, consulted.

---

### Stage 6: Fleet Expansion
**Goal:** Pilot fleet becomes full fleet. One shipping group becomes a fleet programme.
**Customer feeling:** Fleet director: data-confident. COO/CFO: evaluating ROI at scale.
**Critical question:** "Does the per-vessel economics improve at scale? Can I walk into my board with this?"

Key moments: 6-month fleet data review · Expansion business case working session · COO/CFO presentation prepared by CS with fleet director · Second fleet contract signed · Deployment plan for expanded fleet · Second fleet CS onboarding.

**What needs to be true:** The expansion conversation happens because CS initiated it at Month 5 — not because the fleet director asked. The business case is built jointly, with the fleet director's own incident cost data and the pilot fleet's ROI data combined. (See Enablement Pack — Stage 6 for the working session framework.)

---

### Stage 7: Industry Advocacy
**Goal:** Satisfied fleet directors become reference customers and maritime industry voices.
**Customer feeling:** Proud of the decision. Willing to advocate if asked in the right way.
**Critical question:** "How do I explain the value to a peer without it sounding like a vendor pitch?"

Key moments: Reference customer programme invitation · Guest authorship in Fairplay/TradeWinds · Speaking slot at maritime safety conference · LinkedIn case study approval · Peer introduction programme · G2/industry review · Insurance broker referral.

**What needs to be true:** Advocacy must be earned and structured — not assumed or requested prematurely. The fleet director needs their own safety data story before they can advocate for Orca AI's. Give them the story first. The advocacy follows.

### Action 2 — Bridge Officer Adoption Programme (90-Day Protocol)
**Priority: P0 · Owner: CS + Fleet Director · Effort: 2 weeks to design · Prevents: The Safety Culture Wall**

**Pre-Installation (Week -2):**
Fleet director sends an introduction memo to all bridge teams on vessels being equipped. The memo is prepared by Orca AI, sent by the director. Key message: *"This system augments your watch — it doesn't monitor you. Configuration will be adjusted based on your feedback."*

**Installation Day:**
- CS on-site or on video call. First introduction to OOW with Orca AI present.
- Training: 10 minutes. One thing. "When the alert sounds — here's exactly what you do."
- Officer feedback form: 3 questions. What does your watch pattern look like on this route? What alert sensitivity do you prefer? What would make this more useful to you?

**Days 7–30:**
- CS reviews alert acknowledgment rates per vessel weekly.
- Any vessel below 60%: CS calls the fleet superintendent (not the officer — respects hierarchy). "We're seeing some adjustment in alert response on [Vessel]. Is there anything we can configure better for their route?"
- Week 4: Officer feedback compilation → configuration update sent. Fleet director informed.

**Days 30–90:**
- Monthly adoption scorecard per vessel, shared proactively with fleet director.
- Any bridge officer who gives positive feedback becomes a candidate for "Maritime Safety Champion" designation — named in the quarterly report, recognised at the next fleet safety meeting.

**AI prompt (ready to use):**
> *"Write a bridge officer introduction memo for a fleet safety director to send to their crew before Orca AI's navigational awareness system is installed. The vessel is a [Panamax container vessel / VLCC / bulk carrier]. The officer's experience level is approximately 15+ years. The tone should respect their professional authority completely while clearly communicating that this system augments their watch, not replaces their judgment. Maximum 200 words. Do not use the word 'monitoring.'"*

### Action 4 — Quarterly Safety Intelligence Report (Automated)
**Priority: P1 · Owner: CS + Engineering · Effort: 1 week to template, then automated · Transforms: Renewal conversations from emotional to data-driven**

**Report structure (6 sections):**
1. Fleet Summary — installed vessels, system uptime %, total alert volume this quarter
2. Near-Miss Event Log — summary table by vessel, date, risk category, officer response
3. Adoption Scorecard — alert acknowledgment rate per vessel, ranked
4. Industry Benchmark — near-miss frequency for vessel class and primary route (EMSA, Lloyd's actuarial data, Nautical Institute reports)
5. Estimated Cost Avoidance — conservative calculation: [near-miss events] × [average incident investigation cost for vessel class] = floor estimate. Note: excludes major incident avoidance.
6. Next Quarter Priorities — top 3 CS recommendations

**Delivery timing:** 6 weeks before renewal date. Renewal should be a formality after this document lands.

**AI prompt (ready to use):**
> *"I'm building a quarterly safety intelligence report for [Fleet Name], a [vessel type] fleet operator. Here's the data: [paste metrics]. Write a 2-page executive report in the style of a Lloyd's maritime safety audit summary. Include: section headers, a top-line summary paragraph, a vessel-by-vessel performance table, a comparison to industry benchmarks (cite sources), and a conservative estimate of incident cost avoidance. Close with 3 recommendations for next quarter. Tone: CCXP-certified maritime safety consultant, not startup."*
