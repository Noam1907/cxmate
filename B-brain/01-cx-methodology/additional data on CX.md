Your Journey Map is lying to you.
It shows a smooth arrow from "Awareness" to "Retention."  
It highlights a few pain points in red.  
It looks clean. Logical. Organized.  
  
But those red zones stay red month after month.  
  
Not because you haven't identified the problem.  
Because you've mapped the Touchpoint, not the RESPONSIBILITY.  
  
Here's the pattern I keep seeing:  
  
Symptom: High churn during onboarding.  
CX action: Improve the welcome email sequence.  
Reality: Churn is caused by data latency in the backend (Engineering) and a mismatch in expectations set during the demo (Sales).  
  
If your map only shows the welcome email, you're fixing a structural leak with a coat of paint.  
  
If you want to move from "CX Insight" to "Business Impact," you have to stop mapping only what the customer DOES and start mapping who is ACCOUNTABLE for the friction.  
  
Add these 3 layers to your journey maps:  
  
𝟭. 𝗗𝗲𝗽𝗲𝗻𝗱𝗲𝗻𝗰𝘆  
  
For every critical touchpoint, ask:  
“Which internal system or team must perform for this to succeed?”  
  
A smooth onboarding depends on:  
→ Backend data loading fast enough for first use (Engineering)  
→ Demo narrative matching what the product actually delivers (Sales)  
→ Welcome flow guiding the customer to value (CX)  
  
You stop being the CX Department.  
You start being the Orchestrator of the Ecosystem.  
  
𝟮. 𝗥𝗲𝘀𝗽𝗼𝗻𝘀𝗶𝗯𝗶𝗹𝗶𝘁𝘆  
  
Don't just list the department. List the ROLE.  
  
Instead of: "Support owns the ticket."  
Try: "The Product Manager owns the root-cause resolution of the bug that caused the ticket."  
  
Accountability becomes granular.  
The "not my area" shuffle stops.  
  
𝟯. 𝗖𝗼𝗻𝗳𝗹𝗶𝗰𝘁𝘀  
  
Where do two departments have opposing KPIs that hurt the customer?  
  
Marketing is measured on "Lead Volume" (Quantity).  
Success is measured on "LTV" (Quality).  
  
Your map identifies the Strategic Friction that no amount of "empathy" can solve without a leadership trade-off.  
  
🚦 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗖𝗫 𝗟𝗲𝗮𝗱𝗲𝗿’𝘀 "𝗣𝗼𝘄𝗲𝗿 𝗠𝗼𝘃𝗲"  
  
Next time you present a journey map to your peers, don't ask for "feedback."  
Ask for 𝗖𝗼𝗺𝗺𝗶𝘁𝗺𝗲𝗻𝘁 𝗼𝗻 𝗗𝗲𝗽𝗲𝗻𝗱𝗲𝗻𝗰𝗶𝗲𝘀.  
  
For every red zone on your map, name the team that owns the fix.  
Then ask them, in the room, if they're ready to commit to a timeline.  
  
That's the moment your journey map stops being a document and starts being a plan.
